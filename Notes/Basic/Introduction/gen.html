Abacus
16th Century BC
  • Ancient Chinese tool using beads and rods for calculation.
  • Used for basic arithmetic like addition and subtraction.
Example: Wooden/metal abacus.
Foundation for all future calculators.
Pascal's Calculator
1642
  • Invented by Blaise Pascal in 1642 (France).
  • First mechanical calculator for addition, subtraction, multiplication, division.
Example: Pascaline calculator.
First step towards automation in calculation.
Babbage’s Engines
1822-1833
  • Charles Babbage designed Difference Engine (1822) and Analytical Engine (1833).
  • Introduced concepts of input, storage, processing, output.
Example: Analytical Engine.
Babbage is the "Father of the Computer".
Tabulating Machine
1880
  • Invented by Herman Hollerith in 1880 (USA).
  • Used punched cards for data processing.
Example: Punched card tabulator.
Became IBM, revolutionized data processing.
Mark-1
1930
  • Developed by Howard Aiken and IBM in 1930.
  • First electromechanical computer, used in WWII.
Example: Harvard Mark-1.
Bridge between mechanical and electronic computers.
ENIAC
1946
  • Developed by Eckert & Mauchly in 1946 (USA).
  • First electronic general-purpose computer.
Example: ENIAC, EDVAC.
Start of the electronic computer era.
First Gen
1940-1956
  • Vacuum tubes for circuitry, magnetic drums for memory.
  • Machine language only, room-sized, huge electricity, generated heat.
  • Punched cards, paper tape, printouts.
Examples: ENIAC, UNIVAC I, IBM 701, EDVAC
Large-scale calculations possible, but unreliable and expensive.
Second Gen
1956-1963
  • Transistors replaced vacuum tubes, assembly & early high-level languages.
  • Smaller, more reliable, less heat, faster.
Examples: IBM 1401, IBM 7090, CDC 1604, UNIVAC 1108
Computing became more accessible and affordable.
Third Gen
1964-1971
  • Integrated Circuits (ICs) - thousands of transistors on a single chip.
  • High-level languages, operating systems, multiprogramming, time-sharing.
Examples: IBM System/360, PDP-8, Honeywell 6000, ICL 1900
Computers entered universities, banks, and large organizations.
Fourth Gen
1971-Now
  • Microprocessors (entire CPU on a single chip), HLL, GUIs, networking.
  • Desktop, laptop, mobile size, personal computers, GUI, internet, multimedia.
Examples: IBM PC, Apple Macintosh, Intel 4004/8086
Computers became part of daily life, revolutionizing everything.
Fifth Gen
Now & Future
  • AI, Machine Learning, Quantum Computing, Parallel Processing.
  • Natural Language Processing, robotics, self-learning systems, big data.
Examples: IBM Watson, Google AlphaGo, Siri, Alexa, Quantum Computers
Computers now “think” and “learn”, transforming society.
